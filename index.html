Dengue Fever

Classic Dengue · Dengue Hemorrhagic Fever (DHF) · Dengue Shock Syndrome (DSS) | DOH Philippines, 2025 Guidelines

Overview & Epidemiology

Dengue fever – a mosquito‑borne viral disease caused by any of the four dengue virus serotypes (DEN‑1, DEN‑2, DEN‑3, DEN‑4). Transmitted by the bite of an infected Aedes aegypti mosquito. It is endemic in the Philippines, with year‑round transmission and seasonal peaks (July–September).

📊 Philippines Data (2025)
• From Jan 1 to Mar 1, 2025: 62,313 cases (73% increase vs 2024).
• Nationwide, 123,291 cases reported as of June 2025.
• Fatality rate remained low at 0.4% (4 deaths/1000 cases).
• Most affected regions: Calabarzon, NCR, Central Luzon.
Sources: DOH, PNA
🎯 Transmission & Incubation
• Incubation period: 3–14 days (usually 5–7 days).
• Vector: Aedes aegypti (day‑biting mosquito, peak activity early morning and late afternoon).
• Secondary infection with different serotype increases risk of severe dengue (DHF/DSS).
⚠️ Key point: Dengue is a notifiable disease; early reporting and case management reduce mortality.

Clinical Classification (DF, DHF, DSS)

CategoryKey FeaturesManagement Level
Classic Dengue Fever (DF)Sudden high fever (2–7 days), severe headache, retro‑orbital pain, myalgia, arthralgia, rash. No plasma leakage or bleeding tendency.Outpatient (home care)
Dengue Hemorrhagic Fever (DHF)Fever + bleeding tendency (positive tourniquet test, petechiae, gum bleeding), thrombocytopenia (<100,000/µL), evidence of plasma leakage (hemoconcentration, ascites, pleural effusion). Grades I‑II.Hospital admission
Dengue Shock Syndrome (DSS)DHF + signs of circulatory failure (narrow pulse pressure <20 mmHg, hypotension for age, cold clammy skin, rapid weak pulse). Grades III‑IV.ICU / Emergency
WHO 1997 classification (DHF grades): Grade I – fever + positive tourniquet; Grade II – spontaneous bleeding; Grade III – circulatory failure (weak pulse, narrow pulse pressure); Grade IV – profound shock (undetectable BP). Grades I & II = DHF; Grades III & IV = DSS.

Clinical Phases (Febrile, Critical, Recovery)

🌡️ Febrile Phase (Days 1–3)
Sudden high fever (>38.5°C), facial flushing, body aches, headache, retro‑orbital pain, myalgia, arthralgia. May have vomiting, anorexia. Risk of dehydration and febrile seizures in children.
⚠️ Critical Phase (Days 3–7)
Begins at defervescence (fever drops). Lasts 24–48 hours. Plasma leakage occurs → hemoconcentration, ascites, pleural effusion, shock. Warning signs: severe abdominal pain, persistent vomiting, mucosal bleeding, lethargy, hepatomegaly.
✅ Recovery Phase (Days 7–10)
Fluid reabsorption from extravascular compartments; hemodilution (Hct decreases), generalized rash (maculopapular), bradycardia, diuresis. Patient feels hungry and well.
Critical phase is the most dangerous. Progression to shock is rapid; requires close monitoring of vital signs, hematocrit, and urine output.

Warning Signs & Diagnosis

🚨 Dengue Warning Signs (WHO)
• Abdominal pain or tenderness
• Persistent vomiting (≥3 times/day)
• Mucosal bleeding (gums, nose)
• Lethargy, restlessness
• Liver enlargement >2 cm
• Clinical fluid accumulation (ascites, pleural effusion)
• Increasing Hct with decreasing platelet count
→ Immediate hospitalization!
🔬 Diagnostic Tests
• Complete blood count: leukopenia, thrombocytopenia (<100,000), hemoconcentration (Hct ↑)
• NS1 antigen test (day 1–5) – recommended by DOH as front‑line diagnosis in RHU
• Dengue IgM/IgG ELISA (after day 5)
• RT‑PCR for serotyping and confirmation
• Tourniquet test (positive if >20 petechiae/1 inch²)
📋 Tourniquet test: Inflate BP cuff to midway between systolic and diastolic for 5 minutes; count petechiae. Positive test supports DHF.

Treatment & Fluid Management

💊 DF (Outpatient)
• Oral rehydration (adequate fluids: water, oral rehydration solution, fresh juices)
• Paracetamol 10‑15 mg/kg/dose q4‑6h (max 4g/day)
• Avoid aspirin, NSAIDs, ibuprofen (bleeding risk)
• Daily follow‑up until afebrile for 48h
• Educate about warning signs
🩸 DHF/DSS (Inpatient)
• Isotonic IV fluids (0.9% NaCl or Ringer’s lactate) – initial bolus for shock
• Close monitoring: vital signs, Hct q2‑4h, urine output, fluid balance
• Blood transfusion if bleeding with unstable Hct
• Organ support for severe cases (mechanical ventilation, dialysis)
Fluid protocol (DOH): Maintenance + 5% deficit; adjust based on Hct and urine output. Stop IV fluids when Hct falls (recovery phase).

Nursing Care & Complications

  • Assessment: Monitor vital signs q1‑4h (BP, pulse, RR, temperature). Assess for shock (tachycardia, hypotension, narrow pulse pressure, cold extremities). Strict I&O; daily weight. Check for bleeding (petechiae, gum bleeding, melena).
  • Interventions: Administer IV fluids as prescribed; maintain fluid balance. Prevent fluid overload (puffy eyelids, dyspnea, crackles). Provide tepid sponge bath for fever. Mouth care for bleeding gums. Safety precautions: soft diet, avoid IM injections, use soft toothbrush. Emotional support for family.
  • Complications: • Hypovolemic shock (DSS)
    • Hemorrhage (GI bleeding, intracranial hemorrhage)
    • Fluid overload – pulmonary edema
    • Organ failure: hepatic, renal, myocardial
    • Secondary infections
Mnemonic “C.A.R.E.” – Check vital signs, Assess fluid balance, Recognize warning signs, Educate family.

Prevention – DOH 4S Strategy

🪲 Search & Destroy
Eliminate mosquito breeding sites: cover water containers, clean gutters, remove stagnant water, dispose of tires/bottles, apply larvicide.
🧴 Self‑Protection
Use insect repellent (DEET, picaridin), wear long‑sleeved shirts and long pants, use mosquito nets/screens.
🩺 Seek Early Consultation
Consult doctor if fever lasts >2 days, especially with warning signs. Early diagnosis reduces severe dengue risk.
💨 Support Fogging/Spraying
Support fogging only in hotspot areas (imminent outbreak). Not routine; must be coordinated with local health authorities.
Environmental sanitation: “No stagnant water, no mosquito breeding.” Community participation is key.
